## S2.02: Approving Classes {#s2.02:-approving-classes}

**Owner:** Directors · **Last reviewed:** 2025 board (imported from the Hitchhiker's Guide, July 2026) · **Status:** current — when you find something stale, fix it and update this line.

Approving classes (Sourced from How-To’s folder):  
What to look for:

* Content  
  * Is it appropriate for the indicated grade range?  
    * Remember that we can’t guarantee the age of the students in their class, so it’s better to err on the side of caution  
  * Will it sustain an hour-long class? Are they trying to do too much?  
    * Usually most important for new teachers since returning teachers have a better sense of what it means to teach for an hour  
  * Do they intend to teach any of the no-no topics? (listed below)  
    * ACT/SAT prep  
    * Anything related to alcohol/bartending (21 \= CT legal drinking age, 18 \= CT legal bartending age)  
    * Sex-ed  
    * “How to get into college”  
      * This can be worked into a more acceptable class, e.g. “How to craft a resume” or “Writing a Personal Essay” could be good options to suggest depending on what they want to focus on.  
  * Do they want to teach about something that’s currently a contentious/divisive issue?  
    * e.g. Israel/Palestine relations  
* Length  
  * Give 2-hour classes extra scrutiny, since children have a much harder time focusing for 2 hours, and it’s often harder for teachers to plan to effectively use all of that 2-hour chunk of time. (They also make scheduling a pain.)  
    * Some classes, like art/music/theater/improv/dance workshops, science experiments, cooking classes (essentially something that’s very hands-on) work great as 2 hour classes  
* Location  
  * Do they have any specific location requests/requirements?  
    * Is it simple like “need a chalkboard” or “need a room with windows”?  
    * Or will they need to reserve a separate space? (e.g. dance studio, kitchen)  
* Budget (look in the comments/materials sections of the class submission)  
  * Did they ask for a reasonable amount of materials (both in price and amount)?  
  * Remember the $30 is a guideline amount, and if they have a compelling reason to spend more (e.g. art supplies, science equipment) presidents/directors can authorize an increased budget for the class.

What to do with any potential problems:

* Content  
  * Email the teacher **as soon as** the class is submitted and explain your concerns. This can take the form of a flat-out rejection, or you can ask questions or offer suggestions to refocus the direction of a class and confirm it will go well.  
  * Teachers are usually willing to work with us, and often appreciate suggestions about what has or has not worked well in the past (that can be an easy way to “refocus” a class to something you think will be more successful). This applies both to content itself and amount of content/time management.  
    * Mentors/organization leaders know which classes have been taught before and how successful they have been, so talk with them to see if the class has been taught before and has gone okay.  
  * If it’s a contentious topic, follow up to make sure the teacher is aware of potential issues and has plans for conflict resolution. Also confirm they’re presenting the matter as objectively as possible\!  
  * Continue following up/pestering teachers until you get responses and have worked out the problem. Remember that it’s your program, so you have the right to refuse a teacher that is unwilling to work with you to make sure the class is appropriate/well-planned.  
* Length  
  * If concerned about a 2-hour class: Explain our concerns with 2-hour classes, and ask if the teacher would be willing to teach a 1-hour class, at least the first time (if it’s a new class) so they can get a better sense of timing before deciding if it’s necessary to lengthen it. If the teacher really wants a 2-hour class we don’t usually fight them, just make sure to emphasize the extra planning that is necessary.  
  * We only suggest increasing the length of a class to 2 hours if they’re doing hands-on experiments or projects, or if they’re planning on traveling from the classroom to another location (see below). Otherwise, suggest that they simplify their lesson plan to better fit in an hour.  
  * Classes that travel from the original classroom to another space (e.g. Bass computer lab, dance studio, kitchen) should be 2 hours long. Walking to/from the other space and getting set up takes time, and there’s just not enough time in a 1-hour class to do that and also teach a full class.  
* Location  
  * Email the teacher **as soon as** you see their class submission to ensure there is enough time to reserve additional spaces.  
  * They should reserve the space themselves whenever possible. If they don’t have access to one of the nearby residential colleges (Silliman, Hopper, Berkeley) tell them we can reserve the space for them since we have admins in those colleges.  
* Budget  
  * Explain any concerns to the teachers and ask if there are ways they can redesign their class to not require those materials.  
  * For science classes, suggest they ask if they can borrow equipment/materials from departments or labs on campus.  
  * Like location reservations, it should be the teacher’s responsibility as much as possible to secure their materials.

   
If a teacher signs up for a **single** section of a class, **DO NOT** approve their class because we want to ask them to teach multiple sections and/or teach a class for 7-8th graders (middle school classes are highest in demand but lowest in supply, usually). Email them asking them to teach either (1) a middle school section (2) another middle school section.

If a teacher signs up for a class with a grade range 7-12 and **is a new teacher**, email them asking them to teach one 7-8 class and another 9-12 class, because classes with a wide age range can be harder to handle. If the teacher is experienced, we usually let this rule slide, but you should still ask them to teach more than 1 section if they aren’t.

Make a spreadsheet for **special requests/class notes.**

Do not approve a class with more than 2 sections for the same grade range. If the class size is small and the class seems like it will be very popular, exceptions can be made if it seems like the class will fill.
