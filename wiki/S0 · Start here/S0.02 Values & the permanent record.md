## S0.02 Values & the permanent record

**Owner:** Presidents · **Last reviewed:** July 2026 · **Status:** current — when you find something stale, fix it and update this line.

### Why this page exists

Splash at Yale has no permanent staff. Every four years, 100% of the people who know how anything works have graduated. Twice (that we know of — 2023 and 2026) the org has had to run a large archaeology project on its own Drive to rediscover things it already knew. A 2023 Phil said it plainly: *"after a certain point, it's easy to forget and make mistakes… or not know why we do things a certain way."* This page is the fix, written down so it survives us.

### What we owe the kids

Splash exists so that any 7th–12th grader in New Haven can spend a Saturday learning something weird and wonderful from someone who loves it, for close to free. Everything else — the spreadsheets, the tape colors, the pizza math — is in service of that. When a policy question has no precedent, decide in favor of the students' experience and safety, then write down what you decided and why.

### The permanent record: three rules

1. **If it isn't written down, it doesn't exist.** A lesson that lives in one person's head has a shelf life of at most eight semesters, usually two. The wiki is the org's long-term memory; your head is a cache.
2. **The wiki is the single source of truth.** One page per task. If you did something the page didn't cover, add it to the page. If the page was wrong, fix it and update its "Last reviewed" line. Never fork a second doc for something that has a page.
3. **Knowledge capture is scheduled, not aspirational.** The Phil (S0.03) after every program and the transition checklists (S6.02, S6.03) at every handoff are *required steps*, not nice-to-haves. They are how rules 1 and 2 actually happen under real workloads.

### How knowledge flows here

```
run the program  →  Phil (what we learned)  →  wiki edits (make it permanent)
                                            →  next semester starts smarter
```

The failure mode we're guarding against isn't dramatic — it's quiet. Nobody decides to lose knowledge; it evaporates one graduation at a time. The counter-move is equally quiet: fifteen minutes at the end of a Phil turning "we should remember this" into an edit on the page where the next person will already be looking.

### Hard-won values (from ten years of Phils)

- **Directors direct.** In meetings and on day-of, your job is assigning and cross-checking, not doing tasks yourself while the team idles.
- **Never assume competence transferred.** Returning admins forget; new admins never knew. Brief in writing, early, then review verbally.
- **Record your estimates.** Every count you guess (pizzas, maps, lanyards, parent-lounge chairs) gets written down with the actual outcome, so next semester guesses less.
- **Err toward the cautious policy with minors.** No teacher–student direct email, no registering students by hand, no exceptions to full classes — these rules all exist because of specific incidents.
- **Be a good guest.** Facilities, custodians, and Yale police remember us semester to semester. Chalk only where allowed, rooms cleaner than found, notify police of our noisy presence.
